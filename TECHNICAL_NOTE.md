## Technical Note

**Author:** Negin Heidarifard

## 1. Research Question

Do more structured visual representations, combined with a physics-inspired prediction stage, lead to more robust downstream robotic decisions under distribution shift than raw-pixel CNN models?

## 4. Dataset

1800 episodes across 5 splits, generated by `data/generate_dataset.py`:

| Split            | Episodes | Shift axis                |
|------------------|----------|---------------------------|
| train            | 1000     | none (base distribution)  |
| test_base        | 200      | none (in-distribution ref)|
| test_appearance  | 200      | ball / background colour  |
| test_noise       | 200      | Gaussian pixel corruption |
| test_dynamics    | 200      | gravity / restitution     |

Each episode is a dict:
- `input_frames`: ndarray (4, 128, 128, 3)
- `gt_landing_x`: float
- `init_state`: dict with initial (x, y, vx, vy)
- `restitution`: float

Regenerate with:

```bash
python data/generate_dataset.py
```

## 5. Models

Three models were implemented under identical training conditions to isolate the effect of representational structure.

**Model C — Classical baseline (no learning)**
Ball centroid via colour thresholding, velocity from consecutive-frame differences, landing_x via ballistic extrapolation. Provides an interpretable sanity check on task solvability.

**Model A — CNN Baseline (unstructured)**
4 stacked frames (12 × 128 × 128) passed through a 4-stage convolutional encoder and a fully-connected regressor that directly outputs landing_x.

**Model B — Structured Predictor (proposed)**
4 stacked frames are passed through a CNN-based structured bottleneck, followed by a physics-inspired rollout stage to predict landing_x. The goal is to encourage a more compact and task-relevant representation than direct raw-pixel regression.

Train with:

```bash
python models/train.py
```

Training time: ~10 min on CPU (10 epochs, batch 32).

## 7. Preliminary Results

*Preliminary results from the current MVP implementation; single-seed runs, not yet averaged across multiple initialisations.*

### MAE (Mean Absolute Error)

| Split            | CNN Baseline | Structured |
|------------------|--------------|------------|
| test_base        | 0.031        | 0.043      |
| test_appearance  | 0.316        | 0.262      |
| test_noise       | 0.036        | 0.048      |
| test_dynamics    | 0.027        | 0.041      |

### Relative Degradation (MAE_shift / MAE_base)

| Shift      | CNN    | Structured |
|------------|--------|------------|
| Appearance | 10.18x | 6.07x      |
| Noise      | 1.17x  | 1.12x      |
| Dynamics   | 0.86x  | 0.95x      |

### Decision Accuracy

| Split            | CNN    | Structured |
|------------------|--------|------------|
| test_base        | 97.50% | 96.50%     |
| test_appearance  | 52.50% | 52.50%     |
| test_noise       | 97.50% | 97.00%     |
| test_dynamics    | 97.00% | 96.00%     |

---

## 8. Observations

1. **Structured representation reduces appearance-shift degradation at the regression level.** The CNN baseline degrades 10.18x under appearance shift, while the structured predictor degrades 6.07x. This suggests improved robustness at the regression level under appearance shift, although this advantage does not yet translate into better downstream decision accuracy.

2. **Regression improvement does not always translate to decision accuracy.** Under appearance shift, both models collapse to ~52.5% decision accuracy — close to random. This is a useful negative result: it motivates investigating the decision module itself, including threshold calibration and decision-aware training objectives.

3. **Both models tolerate noise and mild dynamics shifts.** Degradation stays under 20% in both conditions. At the current task difficulty, the CNN has enough capacity to absorb these perturbations.

4. **In-distribution cost of structure is small.** Model B trails Model A by 0.012 MAE on the base split — a minor in-distribution tax for a measurable out-of-distribution robustness gain.

---

## 9. Known Limitations

- Single-seed results; no cross-seed variance reported yet.
- The decision layer uses a fixed threshold at scene centre; calibration is not yet performed.
- Task difficulty at the base split is low enough that both models saturate at ~97% decision accuracy, making noise/dynamics comparisons less discriminative than appearance comparisons.
- The environment is a 2D toy setting; generalisation to higher-dimensional or 3D dynamics is not investigated here.
